II.) Foundations of ontology and epistemology in early Greek philosophy

Topics include the ancient Greek foundations of ontology and epistemology in early Greek philosophy; particular emphasis on Platonic and Aristotelian traditions

Required readings

Hergenhahn text: All of Chapter 2; Chapter 19: Karl S. Lashley, especially discussions on the engram; Chapter 13: Tolman, especially "Intervening variables" & "Hypotheses, Expectancies, Beliefs, and Cognitive Maps"; Hull, especially discussions on "Reaction potential"; Skinner, especially "Operant behavior".

  • Plato's Allegory of the Cave
  • Meno's paradox and Plato's view that learning is recollection
  • Psycholinguistics and Plato's paradoxes of the Meno: read from the start through "The Centrality of Aristotelian Thought Today" & last section "Have We Learned Anything about Learning in the Last 2,500 years?"

Helpful reads and misc. items

  • Plato's allegory of the Cave video

  • xkcd comic takes the cave allegory literally (though I suppose this actually has more to do with Descartes than Plato)

  • Newton, physics, and Aristotle's Four Causes

  • Aristotle's de Anima (read Books 1 and 2)

  • Werner Heisenberg's Physics and Philosophy:
    For everyone: on p. 63 (of actual text, not pdf) the author notes the similarities between the claims of the ancient cosmologists (esp. Heraclitus) and modern physics.

    For the mildly adventurous: all of Chapter 4 provides a good overview of early Greek thinking as it relates to developments in physics.

    For the extremely adventurous the book is an excellent diversion and summary of the development and ideas of quantum physics from one of its key proponents.
